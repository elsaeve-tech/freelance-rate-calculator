Why Freelancers Often Undercharge

Many freelancers only think about paid working hours. But in reality, a large part of freelance work is unpaid: emails, revisions, proposals, admin work, marketing, client calls, and follow-up.

That means if you work 30 hours in a week, you may only bill for 15 to 20 of those hours. If you set your prices too low, the math stops working very quickly.

What Your Freelance Rate Should Cover

Your pricing should account for:

• your desired annual income

• monthly and yearly expenses

• non-billable time

• revisions and communication

• taxes, profit, and business growth

Hourly vs Project Pricing

Hourly pricing can be useful for small or flexible tasks, but many freelancers eventually move toward project pricing because it is easier for clients to understand and can better protect your time.

A strong project price usually starts with an hourly or daily baseline, then gets adjusted based on complexity, revisions, speed, and the value of the work.

A Better Way to Figure Out What to Charge

Instead of guessing, start with your income goal and work backward. Estimate how many hours you realistically work, then reduce that number based on how much of your time is actually billable.

From there, you can calculate a sustainable hourly rate and turn that into day rates or project rates.

Freelance Pricing FAQ

Should I charge hourly or per project?

Project pricing is often better for defined work. Hourly pricing can make sense for smaller tasks, consulting, or unclear scopes.

How do I know if I am undercharging?

If your rate looks okay on paper but you still feel overworked, your billable time may be lower than you think. That is one of the most common reasons freelancers undercharge.

What if I am a beginner?

Beginners may charge less at first, but your rate should still support your actual time and expenses. Charging too little can make freelancing unsustainable very quickly.
